Absolute Magnitude is how bright a star would look if every object was placed exactly 10 parsecs (32.6 light-years) from Earth.

This removes the distance trick — now we can compare true brightness. Notice how the ranking changes!

Luminosity is a star's total energy output — its true power. It depends on size and temperature. Drag the distance slider on each star to see how apparent brightness changes, but luminosity stays constant.

The hottest, largest stars are the most luminous. The smallest, coolest stars are the least luminous.
Key Insight: Moving a star closer makes it appear brighter, but its luminosity doesn't change. The Sun looks brightest from Earth only because it is so close — many stars are thousands of times more luminous but appear faint because of distance. Luminosity follows the inverse square law: double the distance = one quarter the apparent brightness.

Electromagnetic radiation is energy that travels through space as waves. All EM waves move at the speed of light (300,000 km/s) but differ in wavelength and frequency.

Shorter wavelength = higher frequency = more energy. This is why gamma rays are deadly and radio waves are harmless.
TAP A BAND TO EXPLORE IT
10⁻¹² m (gamma) → wavelength increases → 10⁹ m (radio)
Key Rule: E = hf — energy equals Planck's constant times frequency. Double the frequency, double the energy. Astronomers study all bands of the EM spectrum to build a complete picture of the universe.
Atmospheric Windows: Earth's atmosphere only lets through visible light and most radio waves. Infrared, UV, X-ray, and gamma telescopes must go to space — that's why we built Hubble, Chandra, and the James Webb Space Telescope.
Multi-wavelength astronomy means studying the same object in different parts of the spectrum. Each band reveals something invisible to the others — together they tell the complete story.
